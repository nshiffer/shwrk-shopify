How to Find & Fix Broken Links in Your Shopify Store

Broken links frustrate customers, hurt your search rankings, and silently kill conversions. Here's how to find every dead link in your store and fix them before they cost you sales.

Why broken links matter.

Every broken link on your Shopify store is a small failure. A customer clicks expecting to see a product and gets a 404 page instead. A search engine crawler follows a link and finds nothing. These add up fast.

SEO impact: Google treats broken internal links as a signal that your site isn't well-maintained. Each 404 error wastes crawl budget that could be spent indexing your actual products. Over time, this means lower rankings and less organic traffic.

Customer experience: Shoppers who hit a dead end don't go searching for the right page. They leave. Studies consistently show that 404 errors are one of the top reasons customers abandon a store. Every broken link is a potential lost sale.

Trust erosion: Broken links make your store look abandoned or unprofessional. This is especially damaging for newer stores trying to build credibility with first-time visitors.

Common causes of broken links in Shopify.

Broken links don't appear out of nowhere. They're almost always the result of normal store operations that nobody thinks to check afterward.

Deleted or renamed products

You remove a product or change its URL handle, but the old link still exists in blog posts, other product descriptions, or your navigation menu. This is the most common source of 404 errors in Shopify stores.

/products/summer-hat-2024404 not found

Archived products

You archive a seasonal or out-of-stock product, but pages and blog posts still link to it. The product exists in Shopify but isn't visible to customers, creating a confusing dead end.

/products/holiday-bundle-2023archived

Draft or unpublished content

You add a link to a page or blog post that's still in draft. Or you unpublish a page but forget to update the navigation menu. The link works in your admin preview but not for customers.

/pages/coming-soondraft

Collection and page reorganization

You restructure your collections or rename pages, but old URLs are still referenced throughout your store content. Navigation menus, product descriptions, and blog posts all accumulate stale links over time.

Manual checking vs. automated scanning

You can check for broken links manually — open every product, page, and blog post, click every link, and see if it works. But this approach has serious limitations:

  • ✗ A store with 100 products, 20 pages, and 30 blog posts can have thousands of internal links. Checking them all by hand takes hours.
  • ✗ You can't check navigation menus by hand — nested menus with dozens of links are easy to miss.
  • ✗ Even if you check everything today, new broken links appear every time someone archives a product or changes a URL.
  • ✗ External link checkers can't see your draft/archived content status — they only know if a URL returns a 404.

This is why automated scanning matters. A tool that understands your Shopify store can check every link, classify issues by severity, and tell you exactly which product description or menu item contains the bad link.

2

Prioritize by severity

Fix 404 errors first — these are the worst for SEO and customer experience. Then address archived product links. Draft content links are usually lower priority unless they're in your navigation menu.

3

Fix 404s with redirects

For deleted products or changed URLs, create a URL redirect. This sends anyone visiting the old URL to the correct page. In LinkGuard, click "Create Redirect" on any 404 issue and it's done.

Republish or update archived content

Preventing broken links.

The best broken link strategy is to prevent them from appearing in the first place. Here are habits that keep your store clean:

  • ✓ Always create a redirect when deleting or renaming a product. Before you remove a product, set up a URL redirect to a relevant alternative. This takes 10 seconds and prevents 404s permanently.
  • ✓ Check your navigation menus after content changes. If you unpublish a page or archive a collection, check your header and footer menus for links that now point nowhere.
  • ✓ Use relative links in your content. When linking between products and pages in descriptions, use relative paths (/products/my-product) rather than full URLs. This makes your links more resilient to domain changes.
  • ✓ Run scheduled scans. Even with the best habits, broken links slip through. A weekly automated scan is the safety net that catches everything else.

Frequently asked questions.

How often should I check for broken links?

At minimum, once a week. If you frequently add, remove, or edit products, daily scans are better. LinkGuard lets you schedule automatic scans so you don't have to remember.

Do broken links really affect my Shopify store's SEO?

Yes. Google uses crawl budget to index your store. Every 404 error wastes that budget. Internal broken links also break the link equity flow between your pages, weakening your overall site authority.

What's the difference between a 404, archived, and draft link?

A 404 means the resource is completely gone — it doesn't exist in Shopify at all. An archived link points to a product that exists but is hidden from customers. A draft link points to a page or article that hasn't been published yet. All three create dead ends for visitors.

Does LinkGuard slow down my store?

No. LinkGuard runs entirely inside the Shopify admin. It doesn't inject any scripts into your storefront. Your customers' experience and Lighthouse scores are completely unaffected.

Can I fix broken links without installing an app?

You can manually check links and create redirects in Shopify admin (Settings > Navigation > URL Redirects). But this doesn't help you find broken links — you need to know which URLs are broken first. That's the hard part that LinkGuard automates.
